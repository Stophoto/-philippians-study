Setting the Scene

A City Built on Climbing

Philippi was a city obsessed with status. As a Roman colony, it functioned like a miniature Rome — complete with Latin as the official language, Roman law, and a social hierarchy that rewarded ambition. Veterans, merchants, and civic leaders competed for honor, titles, and public recognition. Climbing mattered. Your rank determined where you sat, who greeted you, and how you were buried.

Into this culture, Paul writes from prison. He’s been talking about a joy that chains can’t touch (chapter 1) and a life defined by Christ rather than circumstance. Now he turns to the life of the community itself — how they treat one another, what shapes their posture toward each other, and what pattern holds it all together.

The Philippians already know the pressure to climb. What they’re about to hear will challenge everything their city trained them to value.

Observe

What Does the Passage Actually Say?

Before we interpret, we observe. These exercises put you directly in the text with a pen in hand. Work through them carefully — this is where discovery happens.

1. The Foundation: What Has Been Given? (vv. 1–4)

Read verse 1 carefully. Paul lists four realities that believers have already experienced in Christ. Write them out.

3. Map the Reversal (vv. 9–11)

a. List what God does in response to Christ’s descent. What does He give? What is the result?

4. The Hinge (v. 5)

What you’ve been practicing — marking key words, tracing patterns, listing actions in sequence, comparing the beginning and end of a passage — these are tools that work in any passage. You’re learning how to study the Bible, not just Philippians.

Interpret

What Does This Mean?

Now that you’ve observed what the text says, it’s time to ask what it means. The Greek insights below confirm and deepen what you’ve already noticed. Greek terms include a reference number (like G3444) — these link directly to the word’s full entry on BibleHub, where you can explore the original language behind the English. They’re breadcrumbs for further study, not required reading.

1. The Fourfold Appeal: Why Humility Is Possible (vv. 1–4)

You listed four realities in verse 1. Paul grounds everything that follows in what believers have already received — encouragement that comes from being in Christ, comfort that flows from His love, shared life through the Spirit, and visceral tenderness that moves to action. These are experienced realities, not abstract ideals. Paul’s logic is precise: because you have received this, live this way.

If humility is grounded in what we’ve received from Christ — what happens to humility when someone tries to produce it from willpower alone?

The two impulses Paul opposes in verse 3 are sharp. Selfish ambition (G2052) originally carried political overtones — jockeying for position, canvassing for influence. It’s the drive to advance yourself even in good work. Conceit (G2754) literally means “empty glory” — seeking validation where there is no substance. It’s the need to be noticed or admired in order to feel whole. Paul calls it empty because only God’s glory satisfies; everything else is a shadow.

Against these, Paul sets humility — counting others as more significant (G5242). This is a deliberate posture, not self-loathing. It means your worth is already secured in Christ, so another person’s flourishing is a joy rather than a threat.

Paul has named the foundation and the posture. Now verse 5 turns the corner — from the community’s disposition to the person who defines it.

2. The Descent: Christ as the Pattern (vv. 5–8)

You traced Christ’s actions and saw the downward sequence. You noticed that “form” appears twice — once for God, once for a servant.

What does it mean that the same word describes both? If Christ’s “form of God” tells us something about His nature — what does His “form of a servant” tell us?

form (G3444)

The outward expression that corresponds to and reveals inner reality — essential nature made visible. Paul uses it twice: “form of God” (v. 6) and “form of a servant” (v. 7). The same word for both. Christ’s servanthood is just as real and just as revealing of His character as His divine glory. The servant form does not conceal who God is. It displays who God is.

mind (G5426)

More than thinking — a settled disposition, an orientation that governs how you see and respond to the world. Paul uses this word ten times in Philippians. It’s a thread through the entire letter. When he says “have this mind,” he’s pointing to Christ’s entire orientation — downward, others-focused, self-giving — and saying: this disposition is yours to live from.

Christ did not count equality with God “a thing to be grasped” (G725) — something to exploit or leverage. The contrast with Adam is striking: in Eden, Adam reached for godlikeness; Christ, already being God, released His grip. The downward movement begins from strength choosing surrender — divine authority wielded with restraint.

He emptied himself (G2758) — a word that means to pour out or make of no account. This is not subtraction of deity but addition of servanthood. He didn’t become less God. He took on more — the full weight of human limitation, dependence, and suffering. Each step descends further: form of a servant, born in human likeness, found in human form, humbled himself, became obedient to death — even death on a cross. That final phrase is a shudder. Crucifixion was Rome’s punishment for the lowest — slaves, rebels, enemies of the state. For Christ to end there was the most complete inversion of status imaginable.

3. The Vindication: God’s Response (vv. 9–11)

You mapped the reversal and saw the shift: in verses 6–8, Christ acts — choosing descent. In verses 9–11, God acts.

The passage moves from Christ choosing to go down (vv. 6–8) to God lifting Him up (vv. 9–11). What does that pattern suggest about how God’s kingdom works — and how it differs from the way the world works?

The original language sharpens the pattern you just described. The word therefore (G1352) in verse 9 is the hinge. Because Christ descended to the uttermost depths, God elevated Him to the uttermost heights. This is vindication — public recognition of who He always was.

The name above every name is almost certainly “Lord” — the Greek word that translates YHWH, the covenant name of Israel’s God. Paul is making an extraordinary claim: Jesus shares the identity of Israel’s God. And he anchors it in the Old Testament. Verses 10–11 echo Isaiah 45:22–23, where YHWH declares that every knee will bow to Him alone. Paul takes that declaration and applies it to Jesus — the crucified servant is the Lord of all creation.

Apply

How Does This Truth Confront or Shape Me?

This is where the passage moves from the page to the heart. The goal isn’t to generate guilt or manufacture emotion. It’s to create space for the Holy Spirit to press truth into the places where you actually live.

Quiet Reflection

Read through the passage one more time on your own. Then sit with these questions:
1
Where are you grasping — for status, recognition, control, or comfort — instead of releasing? Be specific. Name the situation, the relationship, or the role where your grip is tightest.
2
Paul grounds the call to humility in what you’ve already received from Christ (v. 1). When you serve others, does your service flow from the security of being loved — or from the need to be noticed? What’s the difference between those two in your own life?
3
The passage says Christ’s descent led to His exaltation. But He didn’t descend in order to be exalted — He descended out of love and obedience. Where are you willing to go low only if the payoff is guaranteed?

Prayer

Close with prayer shaped by the passage:
Father, we stand in the shadow of a mystery — that the God of all creation chose to descend. That glory expressed itself in servanthood. That the cross, not the throne, is where Your heart was most fully revealed. Forgive us for grasping when You have already given us everything we need. Forgive us for climbing when You have called us downward. Teach us the mind of Christ — to count others as more significant, to release our grip on status and comfort, and to trust that You vindicate what the world overlooks. Fill us so completely with Your encouragement, Your love, Your Spirit, and Your tender compassion that humility becomes our freedom — not our burden. At the name of Jesus, every knee will bow. Until that day, let us be people who bow first. Amen.
Action Steps

For the Week

These are not busywork. They’re designed to keep you in the passage between sessions — so the text has time to work on you.
Days 1–2

Read and Mark

Read Philippians 2:1–11 once each day. On Day 1, mark every action Christ takes. On Day 2, mark every action God the Father takes. What pattern emerges between the two?

If you only do one thing this week, this is it. Re-reading with a pen in hand is the most important habit this study builds.

Days 3–4

Go Deeper

Focus on one of these words: “form,” “emptied,” “humbled,” or “name.” Spend 10 minutes with it — look it up on Blue Letter Bible. (Go to blueletterbible.org, type “Philippians 2” in the search bar, click the verse number, then select “Interlinear” to see the Greek words behind the English. Try clicking the Strong’s number for the word you’re studying.) Where else does Paul use this word? What does it reveal?

Day 5

Pray the Text

Use verses 9–11 as your prayer — but pray it personally: “At the name of Jesus, let every knee in my life bow — every ambition, every fear, every grasping impulse — and let my tongue confess that Jesus Christ is Lord.” Write down what the Spirit surfaces.

Days 6–7

Read Ahead

Read Philippians 2:12–18 twice. Don’t study it yet — just read. Notice what connects to what you learned this week. Come to next session with one observation and one question.

Take-Home Reading: Amplified Paraphrase

An expanded rendering of Philippians 2:1–11. Not a replacement for the text — a companion that opens up what’s already there. Read it alongside your ESV.

1 So if there is any encouragement in Christ — any steadying, strengthening word that comes from belonging to Him — any comfort from love — the relief and settledness of being held by a love you did not earn — any participation in the Spirit — any shared life in the Holy Spirit who binds you to God and to each other — any affection and sympathy — any of the gut-level tenderness and mercy that moves you toward another person’s pain —

2 complete my joy by being of the same mind — oriented the same direction, seeing through the same lens, shaped by the same Christ — having the same love, being in full accord — joined at the deepest level, not merely cooperating but pulled by the same gravity — and of one mind.

3 Do nothing from selfish ambition — the drive to advance yourself, to jockey for position, to build your reputation even through good work — or conceit — empty glory, the craving to be noticed and admired that feeds on shadows and never satisfies — but in humility count others more significant than yourselves — the settled freedom of someone who has nothing left to prove, free to lift others because they are already held.

4 Let each of you look not only to his own interests, but also to the interests of others. A quiet, devastating instruction. Not “ignore your own needs” — but stop making them the center. Learn to see what someone else carries.

5 Have this mind among yourselves — adopt this very disposition, this orientation of heart — which is yours in Christ Jesus — already given to you in Him, already the shape of the life you’ve entered. What follows is that mind made visible.

6 Who, though he was in the form of God — existing in the full, undiminished expression of divine nature; not resembling God, but sharing His very essence, radiating it — did not count equality with God a thing to be grasped — did not treat His divine status as a possession to exploit, a privilege to leverage, a throne to cling to. Where Adam reached for godlikeness, Christ — already being God — opened His hands.

7 But emptied himself — poured Himself out; not by subtracting deity, but by adding servanthood — by taking the form of a servant — the same word used for His divine form now used for His servant form; His servanthood is just as real and revealing of His essence as His glory — being born in the likeness of men. He entered. He came all the way in.

8 And being found in human form — recognized as fully human, living among us in the vulnerability and limitation we know — he humbled himself by becoming obedient to the point of death — each step a further descent, each one freely chosen — even death on a cross. The lowest death. The most shameful execution Rome could devise — reserved for slaves, rebels, and enemies of the state. He went there. He chose it. The downward path reached its floor.

9 Therefore — because of this, in direct response to the descent — God has highly exalted him — lifted Him to the highest place, beyond all created authority — and bestowed on him the name that is above every name — the name that belongs to God alone, the name that Israel’s God declared no one else would carry.

10 so that at the name of Jesus every knee should bow — in heaven, among the angels and powers; on earth, among every living person; and under the earth, among the dead and the spiritual realm — every created being acknowledging what is true —

11 and every tongue confess that Jesus Christ is Lord — the word that translates YHWH, the covenant name of Israel’s God — to the glory of God the Father. The crucified servant is the Lord of all creation. And this confession does not diminish the Father’s glory — it completes it. The Son’s descent and exaltation reveal the very heart of God.

Key Greek Insights

Selected terms for this passage. These go deeper than the session material. Each entry includes cross-references and a prompt for personal study. Use them alongside Blue Letter Bible during Days 3–4.
The Fourfold Appeal (v. 1)
encouragement (G3874)

A word that means to come alongside — to strengthen, steady, and call someone forward. It can mean comfort or exhortation depending on context. Here it describes what happens to a person who is “in Christ” — they are met, steadied, and given courage to continue. The encouragement is not from Paul. It is in Christ Himself.

Cross-reference Acts 4:36. Barnabas is called “son of encouragement” (same root). What does it look like when a person becomes an embodiment of this word?
comfort (G3890)

Consolation that comes close — a nearness of love that brings relief and settledness. This is love experienced as refuge. Paul is saying: if God’s love has ever brought you rest, let that rest reshape how you treat others.

Cross-reference 2 Corinthians 1:3–4. Paul calls God “the Father of mercies and God of all comfort.” How does this cycle — God comforts you, you comfort others — connect to what Paul is asking of the Philippians in verses 2–4?
participation (G2842)

Shared life, mutual partnership, common ownership. The same word translated “fellowship” and “partnership” elsewhere in Philippians (1:5). Here it describes a shared life with the Holy Spirit — drawn into His presence, participating in His work.

Cross-reference 1 Corinthians 1:9, 2 Corinthians 13:14. Paul speaks of “participation” with the Son and with the Spirit. What is the “shared thing” in each case?
affection and sympathy (G4698) + (G3628)

Visceral tenderness and compassion that moves to action. The first word refers to the inward organs — gut-level emotion, the deep place where care is felt physically. The second word means mercy that responds. Together they describe the emotional texture of a community shaped by Christ — tender, responsive, moved by one another’s pain.

The Call to Humility (vv. 2–4)
mind / mindset (G5426)

To set one’s orientation, to adopt a governing disposition. Paul uses this word ten times in Philippians. It’s the thread that runs through the whole letter. In 2:2 he uses it twice: “be of the same mind… of one mind.” In 2:5 he says “have this mind among yourselves.” This is not about thinking the same thoughts. It is about sharing the same orientation — a way of seeing shaped by Christ’s own posture.

Cross-reference Philippians 3:15 and 4:2. Paul uses this same word for mature thinking and for reconciliation between Euodia and Syntyche. What does it tell you that this one word connects humility, maturity, and peacemaking?
selfish ambition (G2052)

Originally a political word — the self-seeking of a person canvassing for office, jockeying for position, advancing their own cause. Paul places it as the opposite of Christ-mindedness.

Cross-reference Philippians 1:17. Paul used this same word for those preaching Christ from selfish ambition while he was in prison. How does the word connect chapters 1 and 2?
conceit (G2754)

Literally, “empty glory” — the craving for recognition that feeds on what has no substance. Paul calls it empty because only God’s glory satisfies. Everything else is a shadow that vanishes when the light arrives.

more significant (G5242)

To surpass, to hold in higher regard. The posture of counting others as more significant is not self-deprecation — it’s the freedom of someone whose identity is already established. When your worth is secured in Christ, another person’s flourishing becomes a joy rather than a threat.

The Descent of Christ (vv. 5–8)
form (G3444)

One of the most important words in the passage. It means the outward expression that corresponds to and reveals inner reality — essential nature made visible. Paul uses it twice: “form of God” (v. 6) and “form of a servant” (v. 7). The same word for both. Christ’s servanthood is just as real and just as revealing of His character as His divine glory. The servant form does not conceal who God is. It displays who God is.

For further study Mark 16:12 is the only other NT use of this word — Jesus appeared “in another form” after the resurrection. How does that usage inform what “form” means in Philippians 2?
grasped (G725)

Something seized, clutched, or exploited — like plunder or a trophy gripped tightly. Christ did not treat His equality with God as a possession to leverage. He held it with open hands. The contrast with Adam is sharp: Adam reached for what was not his; Christ released what was rightfully His.

Cross-reference Genesis 3:5–6. What did Adam and Eve reach for? What did they expect it to give them? How does Christ’s posture in Philippians 2:6 reverse that impulse?
emptied (G2758)

To pour out, to make of no account, to render void of advantage. Christ emptied Himself — not by subtracting His divine nature, but by adding the full weight of human limitation and servanthood. He did not become less God. He took on more — dependence, vulnerability, suffering. The pouring out was not loss of identity but overflow of love.

Cross-reference Isaiah 53:12. The Suffering Servant “poured out his soul to death.” How does Isaiah’s language illuminate what Paul means by “emptied”?
humble (G5013)

To bring low, to take a lower position voluntarily. In the Greco-Roman world, humility was weakness — the mark of someone who had no choice. Paul redefines it: Christ’s humbling is an act of strength. He descends deliberately, freely, from a position of supreme authority.

obedient (G5255)

Submissive, responsive to authority — literally, to place oneself under the hearing of another. Christ aligned Himself fully with the Father’s will, even when that will led to crucifixion. This is the deepest expression of trust — trusting that the Father’s purpose is worth the cost.

Cross-reference Hebrews 5:8. “Although he was a son, he learned obedience through what he suffered.” What does it mean that the eternal Son “learned” obedience? What does that suggest about obedience as lived experience rather than abstract duty?
The Exaltation (vv. 9–11)
therefore (G1352)

A small word carrying enormous weight. It means “for this very reason” — connecting Christ’s descent directly to God’s response. Because He went to the lowest place, God lifted Him to the highest. The exaltation is vindication — the Father publicly recognizing who Christ always was and what His descent accomplished.

highly exalted (G5251)

A word used only here in the entire New Testament. The prefix intensifies: not merely exalted, but super-exalted, raised to the highest conceivable position. This is not promotion — Christ did not earn a new status. It is revelation — God making visible to all creation what was true from eternity.

Cross-reference Acts 2:33. Peter uses “exalted” (without the intensified prefix) for Christ’s position at the Father’s right hand. How does Paul’s intensified form add theological weight?
name (G3686)

In Scripture, a name represents the person’s character, authority, and presence. The “name above every name” is almost certainly “Lord” — the Greek translation of YHWH, the covenant name of Israel’s God. God bestowed on Jesus the name that He declared in Isaiah belongs to Him alone. This is the revelation that the crucified Jesus shares the identity of Israel’s God.

For further study Explore the “name theology” thread in the Old Testament — Deuteronomy 12:11 (God’s name dwelling in a place), John 2:19–21. How does Jesus fulfill what the temple anticipated — as the place where God’s name and presence truly dwell?
Lord (G2962)

The title that translates the divine name YHWH in the Greek Old Testament. When Paul says “every tongue confess that Jesus Christ is Lord,” he is making the most extraordinary claim in the New Testament: the carpenter from Nazareth, crucified under Pontius Pilate, shares the identity and authority of the God who brought Israel out of Egypt. This confession is the heart of early Christian faith (Romans 10:9).

bow (G2578)

To bend the knee — a posture of submission, worship, and recognition. Paul draws directly from Isaiah 45:22–23, where YHWH declares that every knee will bow to Him alone. By applying this to Jesus, Paul places Christ within the identity of Israel’s one God. The descent to the cross leads to the worship of all creation.

glory (G1391)

Weight, splendor, the visible radiance of God’s character and presence. The passage ends where it began — with God. Christ’s exaltation as Lord does not compete with the Father’s glory. It reveals it. The Son’s descent and the Father’s response together display the heart of the triune God: divine power expressed through self-giving love, and divine glory completed through the vindication of the servant who went lowest.

Cross-reference John 17:1, 17:5. Jesus prays to be glorified with the glory He had “before the world existed.” How does this prayer illuminate the movement of Philippians 2:6–11 — from pre-existent glory, through descent, to restored and revealed glory?
